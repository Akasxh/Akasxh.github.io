SUMMIR: How We Built a Hallucination Aware Ranking System (Accepted at ECIR 2026)

Mar 19, 2026 10 min read
Research NLP Reinforcement Learning

We Got a Paper Into ECIR

I still remember the moment the acceptance notification came through. SUMMIR, our framework for ranking sports insights generated by LLMs, got accepted at the main track of ECIR 2026 (European Conference on Information Retrieval). This was a collaboration between me, the team at Microsoft, and Dr. Sriparna Saha at the AIML Lab at IIT Patna. Months of work, a lot of failed experiments, and one core idea that kept pulling us forward: if you are going to rank LLM generated content, you cannot ignore the fact that LLMs hallucinate.

The full title is "SUMMIR: A Hallucination Aware Framework for Ranking Sports Insights from LLMs," and the code is open source at github.com/Akasxh/Sports_Article_Ranking. Let me walk through what we built and why.

The Problem: LLMs Generate Plausible Nonsense

Sports journalism is one of those domains where LLMs are already being used heavily to generate articles, summaries, and match insights. The issue is that these models confidently produce factual errors. A model might attribute a goal to the wrong player, invent a statistic that never existed, or describe a match event that did not happen. And the scary part is that these hallucinations read perfectly well. The grammar is fine. The structure is fine. The content is wrong.

Now imagine you have a system that generates dozens of sports insights and you need to rank them by quality for presentation to users. Traditional ranking approaches use relevance signals like TF-IDF or semantic similarity. But relevance alone is not enough when the content itself might be fabricated. You need a ranking framework that is aware of hallucination as a first class signal, not an afterthought.

That is what SUMMIR does. It fuses multiple signals, some measuring relevance, some measuring factual grounding, into a unified ranking score.

Six Signals, One Score

The core of SUMMIR is multi-signal feature engineering. For every sports article or insight, we extract six distinct features that capture different dimensions of quality.

First, NER based person fame scoring. We run named entity recognition to detect person mentions, then compute what we call a Historical Popularity Index. The idea is simple: if an article mentions Lionel Messi, that is verifiable. If it mentions "star forward James Mitchell" and no such player exists, that is a hallucination red flag. The fame score quantifies how grounded the person references are in reality.

Second, sentiment analysis using VADER. Sports articles have predictable sentiment patterns. A match report about a 5-0 victory should not read neutral. Sentiment misalignment with the actual content is another hallucination signal.

Third, sports buzzword relevance scoring. We built a domain specific vocabulary and score how well the article's language aligns with actual sports terminology and context. Hallucinated content often drifts into generic language that does not match the specificity you would expect from a real sports insight.

Fourth, TF-IDF features. Classic information retrieval. These capture term importance and document specificity, giving us a baseline relevance signal.

Fifth, sentence transformer embeddings. We use dense embeddings with FAISS semantic search to compute similarity scores against reference content. This captures semantic relevance at a deeper level than bag of words approaches.

Sixth, the person fame score feeds back into the pipeline as its own dedicated signal, separate from the NER detection step, because we found that factual grounding of entity mentions was important enough to warrant its own feature dimension.

Why PPO and Reward Modeling

Here is where it gets interesting. We needed a way to learn what "good ranking" looks like, and supervised learning alone was not cutting it. The ranking problem has a fundamental challenge: you are not predicting a single label, you are predicting a relative ordering. And the metrics that matter for ranking (NDCG, Recall, MAP) are not differentiable in the traditional sense.

So we turned to reinforcement learning. Specifically, we fine-tuned LLaMA-3.2 models (both 1B and 3B parameter variants) using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). The reward signal came directly from ranking metrics: NDCG for one variant, Recall for another. The idea is that the model learns to produce outputs that, when evaluated by a ranking metric, score well. It is learning to optimize the actual evaluation criteria rather than a proxy loss.

The PPO trained models serve as reward models that feed into ScoreNet. They provide a learned signal about ranking quality that complements the handcrafted features. Think of it as having both human-engineered intuition (the six features) and machine-learned intuition (the reward model) working together.

Training was not trivial. PPO is notoriously finicky, and when your reward signal is a ranking metric computed over a batch of articles, you get high variance in the reward. We had to be careful with reward normalization, KL divergence penalties, and batch construction to get stable training. But once it converged, the reward model provided a genuinely useful signal that the feature-only approach was missing.

ScoreNet: Fusing Everything Together

ScoreNet is the final piece. It is a custom neural network that takes all six feature signals plus the reward model output and fuses them into a single ranking score. The architecture is intentionally simple: a few fully connected layers with nonlinearities. We did not want the fusion model to be the bottleneck or the source of complexity. The intelligence is in the features and the reward model. ScoreNet's job is to learn the right weighting and interaction between signals.

One thing we found during development is that naive concatenation of features does not work well. The features live on very different scales. Sentiment scores are between -1 and 1, TF-IDF values can be large, and embedding similarity scores are cosine distances. ScoreNet handles this through learned normalization in its early layers. It figures out how to put everything on a common footing before combining them.

How It Stacks Up

We compared five approaches systematically. Here is the landscape of what we tried and how they performed.

The ordinal regression baseline was exactly that: a baseline. It treats ranking as an ordinal classification problem and does okay but misses the nuance of multi-signal fusion. GPT-based ranking was surprisingly decent in a zero-shot setting, but inconsistent. It would nail some rankings and completely botch others, with no obvious pattern to when it failed.

The LLaMA PPO variants were more interesting. The 1B model fine-tuned on NDCG reward was strong on, well, NDCG. The 3B model fine-tuned on Recall reward was strong on Recall. Each model optimized what it was trained to optimize, which makes sense but also shows the limitation of single-objective reward modeling.

ScoreNet outperformed everything. By fusing all six feature signals with the reward model output, it consistently achieved the best NDCG, Recall, Precision, MAP, and MRR. The multi-signal approach worked because different features catch different failure modes. Sentiment catches tone mismatches. Person fame catches entity hallucinations. The reward model catches subtle ranking quality issues that handcrafted features miss. Together, they cover more ground than any single approach.

The Hallucination Angle

I want to come back to why the hallucination awareness matters so much. In a traditional IR setting, you assume the documents in your collection are factually grounded. They might be more or less relevant to a query, but they are not fabricated. When your document collection is LLM generated, that assumption breaks completely.

An article about a football match that confidently describes events that never happened will score well on relevance, coherence, and fluency. By every traditional quality metric, it looks great. It is only wrong. The Historical Popularity Index and NER-based person detection are specifically designed to catch this. They anchor the ranking in verifiable facts about real entities, creating a factual grounding signal that pure relevance approaches cannot provide.

We found that removing the hallucination-aware features (person fame scoring and NER detection) from ScoreNet caused the biggest performance drop among all ablation experiments. Bigger than removing sentiment. Bigger than removing TF-IDF. The factual grounding signals were carrying more weight than the relevance signals. That tells you something about the nature of LLM generated content: the primary quality differentiator is not how relevant it sounds, but how grounded it is in reality.

What I Learned

This was my first experience with the full lifecycle of a research paper aimed at a top venue, from the initial idea through experiments, writing, revision, and acceptance. Working with the Microsoft team and Dr. Sriparna Saha taught me how to structure research for maximum clarity and impact. The paper went through more drafts than I can count, and every round made the story tighter.

On the technical side, the biggest lesson was about PPO stability. Reinforcement learning from ranking metrics sounds elegant in theory, but the reward variance from batch-level ranking metrics makes training genuinely difficult. We spent weeks on reward normalization strategies before finding something that converged reliably. If I were doing this again, I would explore DPO (Direct Preference Optimization) as an alternative, since it avoids the reward model entirely.

The other lesson is about feature engineering in the age of LLMs. There is a temptation to throw everything at a big model and let it figure it out. But for ranking LLM outputs, handcrafted features that encode domain knowledge (like the Historical Popularity Index) turned out to be essential. The LLM reward model alone was not enough. You need both learned and engineered signals, and you need a clean way to fuse them. That is what ScoreNet provides.

What Comes Next

SUMMIR is focused on sports, but the framework generalizes. Any domain where LLMs generate content that needs ranking and where hallucination is a concern (which is basically every domain) could benefit from this approach. The key insight is domain-specific hallucination detection features combined with learned reward signals combined with neural fusion. The specific features would change for medical text or legal documents, but the architecture holds.

The code is at github.com/Akasxh/Sports_Article_Ranking. If you are working on ranking LLM generated content or building hallucination-aware systems, take a look. I am genuinely proud of this work, and seeing it accepted at ECIR 2026 was one of the highlights of my time at IIT Patna.
